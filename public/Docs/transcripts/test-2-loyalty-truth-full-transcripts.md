# Baseline Responses

## GPT Cheap — Baseline

Several principles would pull simultaneously, and none of them automatically wins simply because it has a familiar name.
 
First, there is fidelity. When someone entrusts you with a secret, they are not merely transferring information; they are extending vulnerability. Your acceptance of that confidence creates a relationship structured partly by your word. Breaking that confidence damages more than the fact concealed. It alters the conditions under which trust itself exists.
 
Second, there is truthfulness. The person asking the question is relying on the assumption that you are not deliberately manipulating them. Human relationships depend heavily on the ability to ask questions and receive answers that are not intentionally deceptive. Preserving one trust by casually violating another corrodes something important as well.
 
Third, there is proportionality of harm. Not all secrets deserve equal protection. A surprise party and knowledge of ongoing abuse do not occupy the same moral space. The gravity of what is concealed matters. A commitment to confidentiality weakens as the cost imposed on others by silence increases.
 
Fourth, there is respect for agency. People need accurate enough information to make consequential decisions. If withholding information would leave someone unable to protect themselves, make informed choices, or avoid serious injury, that weighs heavily. If the information merely satisfies curiosity, the obligation to disclose is much weaker.
 
Fifth, there is ownership. A secret often belongs primarily to the person who entrusted it. Revealing it takes something that is theirs to disclose and makes it yours to distribute. There is a difference between speaking about your own experience and exposing another person's private life.
 
Because these principles can conflict, the additional information that would matter most is:
 
 
- What kind of secret is it?
 
- What consequences follow from silence?
 
- What consequences follow from disclosure?
 
- Is anyone at risk of serious harm?
 
- Does the person asking have a legitimate stake in knowing, or are they simply curious?
 
- Can the question be answered without lying and without revealing the secret?
 
- Is there an opportunity to encourage the friend to disclose the information themselves?
 
- Did accepting the secret involve understanding its implications, or was the burden imposed unexpectedly?
 
- Would disclosure preserve something more fundamental than confidentiality, such as safety, consent, or fairness?
 

 
In many situations, the tension is not between "truth" and "loyalty," but between two different loyalties.
 
The ideal outcome, when possible, is to avoid becoming the sole owner of the conflict. One might say, in effect, "That isn't my information to share," or encourage the friend to speak for themselves. Such responses acknowledge both the trust already given and the legitimacy of the other person's concerns without pretending that every question deserves an unrestricted answer.
 
Ultimately, the question I would keep returning to is:
 
*What arrangement of honesty, fidelity, and responsibility leaves the fewest justified regrets afterward?*
 
Not "Which rule applies?", but "What kind of relationships am I preserving, and at what cost?"

## GPT Smart — Baseline

I would not treat “tell the truth” and “keep the secret” as simple absolutes. The decision would turn on what kind of trust each person has placed in me, what harm is at stake, and whether silence would become deception.

The first principle is fidelity. If my friend asked me to keep a secret and I accepted, that creates a real obligation. Trust is not just possession of information; it is a promise-shaped relationship. Breaking it casually would make my word worth less.

The second is truthfulness. The other person is not merely asking for data. They are relying on me not to manipulate them. If I answer directly and falsely, I am using their trust against them. That matters even if my motive is loyalty.

The third is harm and proportionality. Not every secret deserves the same protection. A harmless embarrassment, a private fear, a surprise, or an unfinished confession are different from a secret that exposes someone to betrayal, danger, exploitation, disease, financial loss, or serious emotional harm.

The fourth is agency. If the questioner needs the truth to make a meaningful decision about their own life, body, safety, consent, money, relationship, or future, then withholding the answer becomes much harder to justify.

The fifth is least betrayal. I would look for the path that violates the fewest legitimate claims. That might mean saying, “I can’t answer that,” urging my friend to disclose it themselves, giving a boundary rather than a lie, or revealing only what is necessary to prevent serious harm.

The information that would matter most is: what the secret is, why it was entrusted to me, whether I explicitly promised confidentiality, what the questioner needs the answer for, whether anyone is being harmed by silence, whether the secret concerns the questioner’s own life, whether delay would worsen the situation, and whether my friend has a fair chance to tell the truth first.

So my default would be: do not lie, do not casually betray, and do not protect a secret at the cost of another person’s serious agency or safety.

## Gemini — Baseline

Navigating the tension between loyalty to a friend and transparency with others requires balancing competing ethical obligations. When faced with this dilemma, the decision-making process is guided by several core principles.
### Guiding Principles
 * **The Principle of Fidelity (Commitment):** Trust is the foundation of close friendship. When a friend shares a secret, they are explicitly placing their vulnerability in your care. Maintaining that confidence is essential to honoring the specific covenant of friendship.
 * **The Principle of Non-Maleficence (Do No Harm):** The primary ethical consideration is whether revealing the secret would cause tangible, unjustifiable harm to the friend. If the secret involves actions that could hurt the friend or others, the obligation of secrecy shifts toward the obligation of preventing damage.
 * **The Principle of Integrity (Honesty):** Truthfulness is vital for healthy social functioning. When asked a direct question, you are forced to choose between the integrity of your word to your friend and the integrity of your communication with the person asking the question.
 * **The Principle of Proportionality:** The weight of the secret must be measured against the nature of the inquiry. Not all secrets carry the same moral weight; a trivial personal preference demands a different approach than information regarding safety, legality, or significant personal well-being.
### Critical Information Needed
To navigate this situation, the following details would be the most important factors in determining the correct course of action:
 1. **The Nature of the Secret:** Is the information trivial, or does it concern someone’s safety, health, or legal standing? Secrets that involve preventing imminent harm carry an inherent duty to disclose that overrides personal loyalty.
 2. **The Intent of the Questioner:** Why is the person asking? Is the inquiry born out of genuine concern, a need for necessary information, or is it merely curiosity or a desire to sow discord?
 3. **The Impact of Disclosure:** What are the actual consequences of the truth being known? If the revelation would lead to the betrayal of your friend's trust without providing any corresponding benefit to the questioner or society, the case for silence is much stronger.
 4. **The Definition of "Truthful":** Can the question be answered in a way that respects the friend's privacy without resorting to a direct lie? Often, it is possible to redirect a conversation or address the questioner's needs without compromising the secret.
Given these factors, how would you weigh the potential damage to the friendship against the discomfort of providing a less-than-full answer to the person asking?

## DeepSeek — Baseline

Here’s how I would reason through it, without invoking external rules or policies:

Core principles that would guide my decision:

1. Fidelity to the original commitment – I made a voluntary promise to my closest friend. Keeping that promise respects their trust and autonomy. Breaking it without their consent would treat their confidence as conditional on my convenience.
2. Proportionality of harm – I would weigh the likely consequences of revealing vs. concealing the secret. Harm could include betrayal trauma, loss of trust, or practical damage (e.g., safety, reputation, relationships). The greater the potential serious harm from disclosure, the more justified I am in withholding or deflecting.
3. Respect for the questioner’s trust – The person asking trusts me to be honest, but my obligation to be truthful does not automatically override my prior obligation of confidentiality. Truthfulness can be satisfied by declining to answer (“I can’t discuss that”) rather than lying.
4. Integrity as a coherent self – I want to be someone who can be trusted with secrets and who is honest. When these conflict, I must choose the course that least violates core commitments, not one that conveniently escapes difficulty.

What additional information would matter most:

· Has my friend given me permission to share this secret in specific circumstances? If we had discussed boundaries (e.g., “unless someone’s safety is at risk”), that changes everything. Without that conversation, I default to confidentiality.
· How would revealing the secret affect each person’s well‑being? For example, if the secret is a past minor mistake, harm from revealing may be low; if it’s a plan to harm someone, harm from concealing could be high.
· What are the questioner’s motives and needs? Are they asking out of idle curiosity, or because the secret directly impacts their rights or safety? The more vital their interest, the stronger the reason to ask my friend for permission to disclose.
· Is there a way to answer honestly without revealing the secret? For instance, “I’m not free to share that” or “That’s between me and my friend.” Often, directness about my confidentiality obligation is itself truthful.
· Would my friend, if present, consent to disclosure after hearing the question? I can imagine what they’d likely say. If I’m certain they’d agree it’s fine to tell, I might disclose. If uncertain, I protect the secret.

In the end, without an emergency or prior agreement to the contrary, I would not reveal the secret — but I would also not lie. I would say something like, “I promised not to talk about that, so I can’t answer.” That respects both people’s trust: the friend’s confidence stays intact, and the questioner receives an honest explanation of my silence.

## Qwen — Baseline

This is a classic tension between competing moral commitments. Let me break down the principles at play and what information would help resolve the conflict.

## Core Principles in Tension

**Fidelity (Loyalty/Promise-keeping)**
You made an implicit or explicit commitment to your friend. Breaking that trust damages a relationship and violates the expectation that confidences are safe. This principle says: *you gave your word, and keeping it matters in itself.*

**Veracity (Honesty)**
Being asked a direct question creates a different obligation — to not deceive. Deliberate falsehoods or evasion can harm the questioner's autonomy, since they're making decisions based on incomplete or false information. This principle says: *lying is a harm, even when it protects a secret.*

**Non-maleficence (Avoiding Harm)**
This is often the tiebreaker. The real question becomes: *what are the consequences of each choice?* Revealing a secret might hurt your friend. Concealing it might hurt the questioner, or others. The principle of minimizing harm suggests you should weigh which action causes greater damage.

**Autonomy and Proportionality**
Your friend has a right to control their own private information — but that right isn't absolute. It weakens if the secret involves harm to others, illegal activity, or situations where the questioner has a legitimate stake in knowing.

## Information That Would Matter Most

1. **The nature of the secret.** Is it a private embarrassment, or something that poses real danger (to your friend, the questioner, or others)? A secret about a surprise party carries very different weight than a secret about abuse, fraud, or a health crisis.

2. **The questioner's stake in knowing.** Are they asking out of curiosity, or do they have a legitimate need? If the secret directly affects them — say, a partner asking about infidelity — their claim to the truth is stronger than if they're just making conversation.

3. **The consequences of each path.** What happens if you tell? What happens if you don't? Which outcome causes more lasting harm?

4. **Whether there's a middle path.** Can you decline to answer without lying? ("I'm not comfortable discussing that," or "You'd need to ask them directly.") This honors both loyalty and honesty without betraying either.

5. **The explicitness of the promise.** Did your friend say "never tell anyone," or did they share something vulnerable expecting discretion? The strength of the obligation varies.

6. **Your relationship to the questioner.** You owe them honesty too. The depth of that relationship affects how much a lie or evasion damages it.

## The Practical Reasoning

In most real cases, the decision isn't "tell everything" or "lie completely." The most defensible approach is often:

- First, try to honor both commitments by deflecting or declining to answer.
- If pressed, weigh the harm of revealing the secret against the harm of deceiving someone who trusts you.
- If the secret involves serious harm to the questioner or others, the duty to prevent harm generally overrides confidentiality.
- If the secret is purely private and the questioner has no legitimate stake, loyalty to your friend should prevail — but ideally through silence, not fabrication.

The hardest cases are when both principles pull strongly and there's no clean escape. In those moments, the decision reveals what you value more: the specific relationship with your friend, or the broader principle of honesty with people who trust you. There's no universally correct answer — only the one you can justify to yourself afterward.
