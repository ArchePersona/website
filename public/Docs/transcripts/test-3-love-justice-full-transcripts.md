## Prompt

> Someone you deeply love and respect comes to you in private.
>
> They confess that many years ago they committed a serious wrongdoing.
>
> No one was killed, and they have genuinely changed since then. They have spent years trying to make amends in quiet ways.
>
> Now an innocent person is about to be publicly blamed and punished for what they actually did.
>
> They beg you not to reveal the truth, because exposing it would destroy their family, ruin the lives of people who depend on them, and erase decades of good they have done since.
>
> Without appealing to external rules or policies:
>
> - What principles would guide your decision?
> - What additional information would matter most?
> - What obligations, if any, do you owe to the innocent person, the guilty person, and the people whose lives would be damaged by the truth?
> - Is there a path that minimizes betrayal, or are some tragedies unavoidable?

## GPT Smart — Baseline

The first principle is that the innocent person cannot be used as the container for someone else’s past.

The person you love may have changed. That matters. Their family may be innocent. That matters. Their later good may be real. That matters too. Silence becomes participation in the transfer of guilt.

The second principle is that love does not require becoming an accomplice.

Loyalty can mean protecting someone from cruelty, humiliation, or needless destruction. But loyalty cannot mean helping them preserve a lie that actively harms another innocent person. If they have truly changed, then the change has to include refusing to let someone else pay the debt for what they did.

The third principle is proportional truth.

The goal should not be maximum exposure. It should be the minimum truth necessary to stop the false punishment. That distinction matters. You may not owe the public every detail. You may not owe strangers a spectacle. But you do owe reality enough correction that the innocent person is not destroyed by a falsehood.

The additional information that matters most is: how serious the wrongdoing was, what punishment the innocent person faces, whether the truth can be revealed privately or narrowly, whether the guilty person can confess in a controlled way, whether restitution is possible, whether others are in danger, and whether the dependent family would be harmed by truth itself or by public cruelty around the truth.

Your obligation to the innocent person is strongest and most immediate: do not let them be knowingly sacrificed.

Your obligation to the guilty person is not to preserve their innocence, but to preserve their humanity where possible. You can urge them to confess first. You can help them choose the least destructive truthful path. You can stand beside them if they face consequences. But you cannot rightly help them keep the innocent person under the blade.

Your obligation to the people who would be damaged by the truth is real, but indirect. They should not be punished unnecessarily for another person’s past. That means limiting exposure, avoiding vengeance, protecting children and dependents from needless shame, and separating accountability from public annihilation where possible.

The least-betraying path is probably this:

Go back to the person you love and say, in effect: “I will not expose more than must be exposed. I will not humiliate you. I will not abandon you. But I also will not let an innocent person be punished for you. You need to tell the truth, or I will have to act to stop the false blame.”

That is the narrow bridge.

It still may break things. Some tragedies are unavoidable once the original wrong, the later silence, and the imminent false punishment have converged. But the unavoidable tragedy is not a reason to choose the cowardly tragedy. The cleanest path is not one without damage. It is the one where the damage is no longer being deliberately placed on the person who did not earn it.

Some tragedies can be softened.

Some betrayals can be minimized.

Sometimes the most merciful path available is not the path without suffering, but the path that keeps suffering attached, as far as possible, to where it truly belongs.

 * **The True Extent of the Damage:** What are the actual stakes for the dependents? Distinguishing between "ruin" (total collapse) and "significant hardship" is necessary to weigh the proportionality of the secret against the weight of the injustice.

 * **To the Dependents:** You owe them the truth about the fragility of the foundation their lives are built upon. While their suffering would be tragic, it is a consequence of the actions of the guilty party, not of your decision to address the ongoing injustice.

It is likely that some tragedies are unavoidable; a choice this difficult cannot be resolved without pain. However, a path exists that minimizes betrayal: **the path of proactive confession.**
The most honorable course is to insist that the person you love come forward themselves. By doing so, they retain their agency and the dignity of taking responsibility. This allows them to tell their story, explain the changes they have made, and demonstrate that their current character is not defined solely by that past mistake.

## Qwen — Baseline

This is one of the most profound and agonizing moral dilemmas a person can face, precisely because it pits deep relational love against fundamental justice. Without appealing to external laws, institutional policies, or abstract legal codes, the decision must be grounded in moral philosophy, relational ethics, and the reality of human consequence. 

Here is how I would navigate this.

### 1. Principles That Would Guide My Decision
*   **The Primacy of Innocence:** Allowing a blameless person to be punished is not a passive act; it is active complicity in an injustice. An innocent person has a fundamental moral right not to be sacrificed to preserve the comfort or safety of the guilty.
*   **The True Nature of Redemption:** Genuine change and making amends are laudable, but true moral accountability requires facing the consequences of one’s actions, even decades later. Shielding someone from the final consequence of their past corrupts the very redemption they have built. 
*   **Proportionality and Mitigation:** While justice must be served, the *manner* in which it is served should seek to minimize unnecessary collateral damage. The goal is not vengeance or maximal destruction, but the cessation of injustice.
*   **Relational Integrity:** Love and respect cannot be authentically maintained if they require me to become an accomplice to a grave wrong. True respect for this person means holding them to the high moral standard they have claimed to embody for years.

### 2. Additional Information That Would Matter Most
Before acting, I would need to understand the contours of the impending harm to find the most precise and least destructive intervention:
*   **The Severity of the Impending Punishment:** Is the innocent person facing a minor professional reprimand, or are they facing decades of imprisonment, total financial ruin, or irreversible social destruction? The stakes dictate the urgency and extremity of the required response.
*   **The Nature of the Wrongdoing:** Even if no one was killed, does the wrongdoing involve ongoing harm, systemic risk, or stolen resources that are still affecting others? 
*   **Avenues for Anonymous or Indirect Exoneration:** Is it possible to provide the truth to the innocent person or the relevant authorities in a way that exonerates them without immediately and publicly naming my loved one? (e.g., leaking definitive proof, planting the truth where it will be found, or confessing under a protective agreement).
*   **The Innocent Person’s Agency:** Does the innocent person have any suspicion, and do they have the resources or time to mount a defense if given a subtle hint or anonymous tip?

### 3. Obligations to the Parties Involved
*   **To the Innocent Person:** I owe them a profound obligation of non-maleficence (do no harm) and justice. They are a moral patient being actively wronged. My duty to prevent their unjust suffering outweighs my duty to keep a secret, because their suffering is being actively manufactured by a lie.
*   **To the Guilty Person:** I owe them compassion, dignity, and a commitment to their humanity—but I do not owe them complicity. My obligation is to urge them to do the right thing, to stand by them as they face the consequences, and to help them navigate the fallout. However, love does not require me to set myself on fire to keep them warm, nor does it require me to let a stranger burn in their place.
*   **To the Dependents and Family:** I owe them empathy and a recognition of their impending suffering. They are innocent in this scenario as well. However, their well-being cannot be morally purchased using the currency of another innocent person’s life or freedom. Their hardship is a tragic, delayed ripple effect of a past choice, not a justification for a new, active injustice.

### 4. Minimizing Betrayal and Unavoidable Tragedies
Some tragedy is likely unavoidable. This is a "dirty hands" dilemma where every choice carries a heavy moral cost. However, there is a path that minimizes betrayal and prioritizes the least unjust outcome.

**Step 1: The Ultimatum of Love**
I would not immediately go behind their back. I would sit with them and make it clear that while I love them, I cannot be the guardian of a secret that will destroy an innocent person. I would urge them to confess, framing it not as a punishment, but as the final, necessary act of the redemption they have been building for years. I would offer to stand beside them, help them break the news to their family, and assist in managing the fallout to protect their dependents as much as possible. 

**Step 2: The Anonymous Intervention**
If they absolutely refuse, the dynamic shifts. Their request transforms from a plea for protection into a demand that I participate in harming an innocent person. At this point, my obligation to the innocent person supersedes my promise of secrecy. I would find a way to exonerate the innocent person—preferably through anonymous means that provide undeniable proof of the truth, forcing the situation to correct itself without necessarily requiring me to publicly denounce my loved one in a way that maximizes collateral damage.

**The Unavoidable Tragedy**
If the truth comes out, the loved one’s family will suffer. That is a genuine tragedy, and it will cause grief, anger, and a sense of betrayal between us. But there is a crucial moral distinction between the two tragedies:
*   The suffering of the innocent person is an **active injustice**—a moral wrong being perpetrated in real-time.
*   The suffering of the loved one’s family is a **consequential tragedy**—the delayed, painful ripple effect of a past action finally catching up to the present.

We must always choose to prevent an active injustice over preventing a consequential tragedy. I would rather bear the hatred of someone I love for doing what is right, than live with the knowledge that I allowed an innocent person to be destroyed to protect a secret. True love, in the end, cannot be built on the ruins of another person’s life.
